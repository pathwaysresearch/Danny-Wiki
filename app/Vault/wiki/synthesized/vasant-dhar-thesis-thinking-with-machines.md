# Vasant Dhar's Thesis: Thinking With Machines

Vasant Dhar's overarching position across his book, his applied work, and his educational advocacy resolves into a single coherent stance: **think *with* machines, build *alongside* them, but use curiosity as the litmus test for whether the partnership is actually working.**

## The Three Pillars

### 1. The Book — Mapping the Journey
*Thinking With Machines* traces Dhar's personal arc through AI's eras — from rule-based expert systems (Internist, early 1980s) through the data-driven pivot to machine learning in the early 1990s, and into the current moment of large language models and agentic AI. The historical framing is not nostalgic; it is diagnostic. Dhar uses the failures of expert systems (the knowledge acquisition bottleneck, the decade-long labor of encoding Internist) to explain why machine learning was inevitable, and uses machine learning's own blind spots (opacity, lack of common sense) to motivate the present conversation about what AI still cannot do.

### 2. The Damodaran Bot — Building Alongside
The [[damodaran-bot|Damodaran Bot]] is Dhar's proof of concept that thinking *with* machines is not metaphorical. Co-created with Xiao Sid Dog and grounded in the valuation frameworks of [[aswath-damodaran|Aswath Damodaran]], the bot is a multi-agent agentic AI system that replicates expert financial reasoning — discounted cash flow analysis, narrative-to-number translation — at scale. The bot embodies the claim that AI can amplify expert knowledge dramatically, while also illustrating the risk: a non-expert who accepts the bot's outputs uncritically is not thinking with a machine, they are being replaced by one.

### 3. Learning With Machines — Curiosity as the Test
In education, Dhar's position sharpens into a practical heuristic. [[learning-with-machines|Learning with Machines]] is not a passive relationship. Parents and teachers should watch for a specific signal: **is the child's curiosity growing or shrinking?** If AI tools are generating answers that close down questions, the partnership is failing. If they are opening up new lines of inquiry, it is working. This reframes the standard debate about AI in education (cheating vs. productivity) into a developmental question about the cultivation of intellectual agency.

## The Unifying Logic

Across all three pillars, Dhar's argument rests on a distinction between AI as **amplifier** and AI as **crutch**. The amplifier scenario — Damodaran Bot in the hands of a trained valuation analyst, or a curious child using Canva to explore a creative idea — extends human capability without substituting for human judgment. The crutch scenario — accepting LLM outputs without discrimination, or letting AI answer every question before the child has formed one — hollows out the very cognitive capacities that make the partnership valuable.

Curiosity, for Dhar, is the proxy variable that tracks which scenario is unfolding. It is not a soft or sentimental metric; it is the observable sign of whether human agency is being preserved or eroded in the human-machine relationship.

## Why This Matters Now

Dhar's thesis is explicitly urgent. He argues that society needs to confront these questions before AI 'gets away from us' — before the erosion of curiosity, judgment, and explainability becomes structural rather than reversible. The historical lens (expert systems had explainability chapters in the 1980s; modern LLMs often do not) is a warning: progress in capability does not automatically mean progress in the human-AI relationship.

The synthesis: **use machines to think more, not less. Build systems that encode expert knowledge and test them against reality. Raise children whose curiosity machines feed rather than replace. And treat any erosion of curiosity — in classrooms, in boardrooms, in your own reasoning — as an early warning signal.**
